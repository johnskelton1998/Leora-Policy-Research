## 1. MANAGEMENT SAFETY POLICY STATEMENT

### Policy

Leora Home Health is committed to providing a safe and healthful work environment for all employees. The safety and health of our employees is our first priority. We believe that all workplace injuries and illnesses are preventable through proper planning, training, and adherence to safe work practices.

### Management Commitment

Management pledges to:

1. Provide and maintain a safe work environment
2. Establish and enforce safety rules and procedures
3. Provide necessary personal protective equipment at no cost to employees
4. Ensure all employees receive comprehensive safety training
5. Investigate all accidents and near-misses to prevent recurrence
6. Comply with all applicable federal, state, and local safety regulations
7. Continuously improve our safety program based on employee feedback and industry best practices
8. Lead by example in following all safety procedures

### Employee Responsibilities

All employees are responsible for:

1. Following all safety rules, policies, and procedures
2. Using provided personal protective equipment properly
3. Reporting all hazards, unsafe conditions, and near-misses immediately
4. Reporting all work-related injuries and illnesses promptly
5. Participating in safety training programs
6. Attending regular safety meetings
7. Suggesting improvements to workplace safety
8. Looking out for the safety of coworkers and clients

### Accountability

Compliance with safety policies is a condition of employment. Violations of safety rules may result in disciplinary action up to and including termination. However, employees will never be disciplined for reporting injuries, near-misses, or safety concerns.

## 3. PATIENT HANDLING AND LIFTING SAFETY

### Purpose

To prevent back injuries, strains, and other musculoskeletal injuries related to patient handling and lifting activities.

### Scope

This policy applies to all employees who provide direct patient care involving lifting, transferring, repositioning, or ambulating patients.

### Policy Statement

Leora Home Health recognizes that patient handling is the #1 cause of injuries in home health care. We are committed to preventing these injuries through proper techniques, equipment, and training.

### General Safe Patient Handling Principles

**NEVER lift or transfer a patient alone if:**
- Patient weighs more than 50 pounds and requires full lift
- Patient is unable to bear weight
- Patient has unpredictable behavior
- Patient has a history of falling
- You feel uncomfortable or unsafe

**ALWAYS:**
- Assess patient's mobility level before attempting transfer
- Use mechanical lifts when available
- Request assistance when needed
- Maintain good body mechanics
- Communicate clearly with patient during transfer

### Body Mechanics Best Practices

1. **Plan Ahead**
   - Assess the patient's ability to assist
   - Clear the path of obstacles
   - Position equipment before starting
   - Ensure adequate lighting

2. **Proper Stance**
   - Stand with feet shoulder-width apart
   - Keep one foot slightly ahead of the other
   - Bend at the knees, not the waist
   - Keep your back straight

3. **During the Lift**
   - Hold the patient close to your body
   - Use your leg muscles to lift, not your back
   - Avoid twisting while lifting
   - Pivot your feet to turn
   - Move slowly and smoothly

4. **Communication**
   - Explain what you're going to do
   - Give clear instructions to the patient
   - Count "1, 2, 3" before lifting
   - Ask patient to notify you of pain or dizziness

### Transfer Techniques by Patient Mobility Level

#### Level 1: Independent (No Assistance Needed)
- **Description:** Patient can safely transfer without help
- **Caregiver Role:** Standby supervision only
- **Equipment:** None required
- **Technique:** Allow patient to perform transfer; watch for safety

#### Level 2: Minimal Assistance (Patient Bears Most Weight)
- **Description:** Patient needs minor help with balance or guidance
- **Caregiver Role:** Light touch for guidance and confidence
- **Equipment:** Gait belt recommended
- **Technique:**
  1. Apply gait belt securely around patient's waist
  2. Stand to patient's weak side if applicable
  3. Allow patient to do the work
  4. Provide stability and verbal cues

#### Level 3: Moderate Assistance (Patient Bears Some Weight)
- **Description:** Patient can help but needs significant support
- **Caregiver Role:** Provide substantial physical assistance
- **Equipment:** Gait belt required; consider transfer board
- **Technique:**
  1. Apply gait belt
  2. Position yourself properly
  3. Rock patient forward to standing position
  4. Pivot together to destination
  5. Lower slowly into seat
  - **Two-person assist may be needed**

#### Level 4: Maximum Assistance (Patient Bears Little Weight)
- **Description:** Patient can provide minimal help
- **Caregiver Role:** Perform most of the work
- **Equipment:** Gait belt required; mechanical lift strongly recommended
- **Technique:**
  - **STOP:** Assess if mechanical lift is needed
  - **ALWAYS use two-person assist**
  - Consider whether transfer is necessary or can be delayed until appropriate equipment is available

#### Level 5: Total Assistance (Patient Cannot Help)
- **Description:** Patient cannot bear weight or assist
- **Caregiver Role:** Full mechanical lift required
- **Equipment:** Hoyer lift or similar mechanical lift device
- **Technique:**
  - **DO NOT attempt manual lift**
  - Use mechanical lift according to manufacturer instructions
  - Two-person assist required for safety
  - If lift not available, reschedule non-essential transfers or request equipment

### Required Transfer Equipment

**Gait Belts:**
- Must be available for all patient transfers (Level 2-4)
- Proper width: 2 inches minimum
- Apply snugly around waist, not chest or abdomen
- Check for wear and tear before each use

**Transfer Boards (Sliding Boards):**
- Use for bed-to-chair or chair-to-chair transfers
- Good for patients who cannot stand but can sit
- Reduces caregiver lifting force

**Mechanical Lifts (Hoyer Lifts):**
- Required for patients who cannot bear weight (Level 5)
- Two caregivers required for operation
- Training required before use
- Annual recertification
- Coordinate with patient/family to ensure lift is available in home

### Client Home Assessment for Safe Transfers

Before first visit, assess:
- [ ] Is bed height appropriate? (knee-height ideal)
- [ ] Are chairs stable and have arms?
- [ ] Is wheelchair in good working condition?
- [ ] Is there adequate space for safe transfers?
- [ ] Is floor surface non-slip?
- [ ] Is lighting adequate?
- [ ] Are there grab bars in bathroom?
- [ ] Is mechanical lift available if needed?
- [ ] Are there obstacles that need to be moved?

**Document assessment in patient care plan.**

### Two-Person Lift Requirements

**Two caregivers are required when:**
- Patient weighs over 150 pounds and needs moderate-to-maximum assistance
- Patient has history of falls or unpredictable movements
- Patient is unable to follow directions
- Patient requires transfer in cramped or awkward space
- Single caregiver feels unsafe performing transfer alone

**Procedure:**
1. Designate one caregiver as "lead" who gives instructions
2. Both caregivers review plan before starting
3. Use count of "1, 2, 3" to coordinate movement
4. Lead caregiver provides verbal cues throughout
5. Either caregiver can call "STOP" if they feel unsafe

### Prohibited Practices

**NEVER:**
- Lift a patient by grasping under their arms (axilla)
- Twist your spine while lifting or holding a patient
- Lift a patient who exceeds your capability alone
- Rush a transfer
- Transfer a patient without proper assessment
- Attempt transfer if patient is non-compliant or combative
- Use broken or defective equipment

### Special Considerations

**Bariatric Patients (over 300 lbs):**
- Always use mechanical lift
- Two-person minimum
- Specialized equipment may be required
- Consult with clinical supervisor before first visit

**Patients with Dementia/Confusion:**
- Approach from front where patient can see you
- Use calm, simple instructions
- Allow extra time
- Be prepared for unpredictable movements
- Consider two-person assist for safety

**Post-Surgical Patients:**
- Review surgical precautions (hip replacement, spinal surgery, etc.)
- Follow physician restrictions
- Use extra caution around surgical sites
- Allow patient to direct speed of movement

### Reporting Requirements

**Report immediately if:**
- You feel pain or strain during or after a patient transfer
- A patient falls or has a near-fall during transfer
- Equipment is damaged or malfunctioning
- You identify an unsafe transfer situation
- You need two-person assist but none is available

### Training Requirements

All clinical staff must complete:
- Initial training on safe patient handling (before first patient contact)
- Hands-on transfer technique practice
- Annual refresher training
- Additional training if new equipment is introduced

**Documented Training Required:**
- Body mechanics
- Gait belt use
- Transfer board use
- Mechanical lift operation (if applicable)
- Patient assessment for mobility level

### Handling Identified Hazards

**Low-Level Hazards** (throw rugs, clutter, lighting)
- Communicate with client/family
- Request remediation
- Document conversation
- Re-assess at next visit

**Moderate Hazards** (structural issues, pet concerns, missing safety equipment)
- Document specifically in care plan
- Require family/client to address before continuing service
- Notify clinical supervisor
- Provide reasonable timeframe for correction
- Re-assess before resuming services

**Serious Hazards** (imminent danger, severe unsanitary conditions, threats of violence)
- Immediately notify clinical supervisor
- May refuse service until corrected
- Document thoroughly
- Consider discharge from services if not corrected
- Employee safety is priority

### Weather-Related Hazards

**Adverse Weather Policy:**

Employees are not required to report to client homes when:
- Roads are officially closed or travel not advised
- Severe weather warnings are in effect (tornado, severe thunderstorm, ice storm)
- Conditions make travel unsafe in employee's judgment

**Procedure:**
1. Employee contacts supervisor as soon as unsafe conditions are apparent
2. Supervisor contacts client to reschedule or arrange alternative care
3. Employee safety is priority over service delivery
4. No discipline for weather-related service delays

**Winter Weather:**
- Clients/families responsible for clearing walkways and steps
- Employees should wear appropriate footwear with good traction
- Use handrails on all steps
- Take small steps on potentially icy surfaces
- Notify supervisor if client has not cleared access
- Do not risk injury to access home

### Infection Control in Client Homes

**Minimum Requirements:**
- Running water (hot and cold) for hand washing
- Soap available
- Clean towels or paper towels for drying
- If home lacks these, use hand sanitizer and discuss with supervisor

**Unsanitary Conditions:**
If home conditions present infection risk to employee or patient:
- Document specific concerns
- Discuss with client/family
- Notify clinical supervisor
- May require services be modified or suspended until corrected
- Severe unsanitary conditions may result in discharge from services

### Pet Safety

**General Pet Policy:**
- Pets must be secured in another room or restrained during visit
- Employees are not required to provide care if aggressive pet is present
- Pet waste must be cleaned from care areas

**If Pet is Aggressive or Unsafe:**
1. Request owner secure pet
2. If owner unable/unwilling, discontinue visit
3. Notify supervisor immediately
4. Document incident
5. Future visits may require pet be secured before employee enters

**Animal Bites:**
- Immediately wash wound with soap and water
- Seek medical attention
- Report to supervisor immediately
- Obtain pet's rabies vaccination information from owner
- Complete incident report
- Follow up with physician recommendations

### Hoarding Conditions

**Definition:** Accumulation of excessive items creating unsafe or unsanitary conditions

**Assessment:**
- Pathways blocked or severely narrowed
- Excessive clutter creating fire hazard
- Inability to access needed areas
- Unsanitary conditions due to accumulation
- Pest infestation

**Protocol:**
1. Document conditions with photos if possible (with supervisor approval)
2. Notify clinical supervisor immediately
3. Discuss concerns with client/family
4. May require services be modified or suspended
5. Consider social services referral
6. Employee safety is priority

### Right to Refuse Unsafe Service

**Policy:** Employees have the right to refuse service if they believe conditions present an imminent danger to their safety.

**Procedure:**
1. Employee attempts to communicate concern with client/family
2. If issue cannot be immediately resolved, employee leaves premises
3. Employee contacts supervisor immediately
4. Document situation thoroughly
5. Supervisor makes determination about future services
6. No discipline for good-faith safety refusal

### Documentation Requirements

All home safety concerns must be documented:
- Initial assessment in client record
- Updates when conditions change
- Communications with client/family about hazards
- Actions taken to address hazards
- Any refused services due to safety concerns

## 5. INFECTION CONTROL AND BLOODBORNE PATHOGENS

### Purpose

To protect employees from exposure to infectious diseases, particularly bloodborne pathogens, in accordance with OSHA standards.

### Scope

All employees providing direct patient care are covered by this policy.

### Regulatory Compliance

This policy complies with:
- OSHA Bloodborne Pathogen Standard (29 CFR 1910.1030)
- CDC Standard Precautions
- Texas Department of State Health Services requirements

### Standard Precautions

**Definition:** Treating all blood and body fluids as potentially infectious.

**Standard Precautions Apply to Contact With:**
- Blood
- All body fluids, secretions, and excretions (except sweat)
- Non-intact skin
- Mucous membranes

### Hand Hygiene

**When to Wash Hands:**
- Before and after each patient contact
- Before putting on gloves
- Immediately after removing gloves
- After contact with blood or body fluids
- After touching contaminated items
- Before preparing or administering medications
- Before preparing or serving food
- After using the restroom
- After coughing, sneezing, or blowing nose

**Proper Hand Washing Technique:**
1. Wet hands with clean running water (warm preferred)
2. Apply soap and lather well
3. Scrub all surfaces for at least 20 seconds:
   - Palms and back of hands
   - Between fingers
   - Under fingernails
   - Wrists
4. Rinse thoroughly under running water
5. Dry with clean paper towel or air dryer
6. Turn off faucet with paper towel

**Alcohol-Based Hand Sanitizer:**
- Use when hands are not visibly soiled
- Apply to palm of one hand
- Rub hands together covering all surfaces
- Continue rubbing until hands are dry
- Minimum 60% alcohol content

**Fingernail Requirements:**
- Keep nails short and clean
- No artificial nails or extenders
- No chipped nail polish

### Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)

**General Requirements:**
- Employer provides all PPE at no cost
- PPE must be accessible and available
- Employees must wear appropriate PPE for task
- PPE must be removed before leaving work area
- Employer responsible for cleaning, laundering, disposal

**Gloves:**

**When to Wear:**
- Any contact with blood or body fluids
- Contact with mucous membranes
- Contact with non-intact skin
- Handling contaminated items or surfaces
- Performing venipuncture or finger sticks
- Wound care

**Glove Requirements:**
- Single-use disposable gloves
- Appropriate size for proper fit
- Latex-free options available for allergies
- Never wash or reuse disposable gloves
- Change gloves:
  - Between patients
  - When torn or punctured
  - After contact with contaminated material
  - When moving from dirty to clean task on same patient

**Proper Glove Removal:**
1. Grasp outside of glove at wrist
2. Peel away from hand, turning inside out
3. Hold removed glove in gloved hand
4. Slide fingers of ungloved hand under wrist of remaining glove
5. Peel off from inside, creating a bag for both gloves
6. Dispose in appropriate waste container
7. Wash hands immediately

**Gowns/Aprons:**

**When to Wear:**
- When clothing may be contaminated with blood or body fluids
- During procedures likely to generate splashes or sprays
- When providing care to patients with highly infectious conditions

**Requirements:**
- Fluid-resistant material
- Cover torso from neck to knees
- Sleeves to end of wrists
- Remove and dispose after use
- Do not wear outside of patient care area

**Masks and Eye Protection:**

**When to Wear:**
- When splashes or sprays of blood/body fluids are likely
- During aerosol-generating procedures
- When caring for patients with respiratory infections
- As required during infectious disease outbreaks (e.g., COVID-19)

**Requirements:**
- Surgical mask or N95 respirator (as appropriate)
- Eye protection (goggles or face shield)
- Must fully cover nose and mouth
- Must not be touched during use
- Remove carefully without contaminating self

**CPR Barrier Devices:**
- Always use pocket mask or face shield for rescue breathing
- Never perform mouth-to-mouth without barrier device

### Sharps Safety

**Needles and Sharps Handling:**

**NEVER:**
- Recap needles by two-handed method
- Bend or break needles
- Remove needles from disposable syringes
- Handle broken glass with bare hands

**ALWAYS:**
- Use safety-engineered sharps devices when available
- Activate safety feature immediately after use
- Dispose immediately in sharps container
- Never reach into sharps container
- Close and replace sharps container when 3/4 full
- Use puncture-resistant gloves when handling broken glass

**Sharps Containers:**

**Requirements:**
- Closable, puncture-proof, leak-proof
- Red in color or labeled with biohazard symbol
- Easily accessible where sharps are used
- Replaced when 3/4 full (never overfill)
- Sealed and disposed per regulations

**Location in Client Homes:**
- Located in area where sharps are used
- Out of reach of children and confused persons
- Secured to prevent tipping
- Client/family responsible for proper disposal
- Employee ensures container available and appropriate

### Exposure Control

**Exposure Determination:**

**Job Classifications with Potential Exposure:**
- Registered Nurses - Category I (High Risk)
- Licensed Vocational Nurses - Category I (High Risk)
- Certified Home Health Aides - Category I (High Risk)
- Personal Care Attendants - Category II (Moderate Risk)
- Administrative/Scheduling Staff - Category III (No Exposure Expected)

**Category I Tasks (Exposure Expected):**
- Wound care
- Injections and IV therapy
- Blood glucose monitoring
- Catheter care
- Ostomy care
- Incontinence care
- Bathing and hygiene assistance

### Hepatitis B Vaccination

**Policy:** Hepatitis B vaccination series is offered to all employees with potential occupational exposure at no cost.

**Timeline:**
- Offered within 10 days of initial assignment
- Three-dose series (0, 1 month, 6 months)
- Antibody testing offered post-vaccination
- Booster doses per CDC recommendations

**Employee Options:**
- Accept vaccination (recommended)
- Decline vaccination (must sign declination form)
- May request vaccination later at no cost

**Declination Statement:**
> "I understand that due to my occupational exposure to blood or other potentially infectious materials, I may be at risk of acquiring hepatitis B virus (HBV) infection. I have been given the opportunity to be vaccinated with hepatitis B vaccine at no charge to myself. However, I decline hepatitis B vaccination at this time. I understand that by declining this vaccine, I continue to be at risk of acquiring hepatitis B, a serious disease. If in the future I continue to have occupational exposure to blood or other potentially infectious materials and I want to be vaccinated with hepatitis B vaccine, I can receive the vaccination series at no charge to me."

### Exposure Incident Procedure

**Definition:** Contact of blood or body fluids with eye, mouth, other mucous membrane, non-intact skin, or through needlestick or cut.

**Immediate Actions:**
1. **Wash exposed area immediately:**
   - Needle sticks/cuts: Wash with soap and water
   - Mucous membranes: Flush with water or saline
   - Eyes: Flush with water or saline for 15 minutes
2. **Report immediately to supervisor** (do not delay)
3. **Seek medical evaluation within 2 hours**
4. **Complete incident report**

**Employer Responsibilities:**
1. Document circumstances of exposure
2. Identify source individual if possible
3. Arrange immediate medical evaluation
4. Provide employee with:
   - Copy of OSHA Bloodborne Pathogen Standard
   - Description of employee's job duties
   - Documentation of exposure route
   - All relevant medical records
5. Ensure medical evaluation includes:
   - Testing of source individual (if consent obtained)
   - Testing of exposed employee (if employee consents)
   - Post-exposure prophylaxis per CDC guidelines
   - Counseling
   - Evaluation of reported illnesses

**Confidentiality:**
- All medical information kept confidential
- Only necessary personnel informed
- Source individual information protected

**Post-Exposure Follow-Up:**
- Baseline testing immediately
- Follow-up testing at 6 weeks, 3 months, 6 months
- Monitoring for signs/symptoms of infection
- Referral for ongoing care if needed

### Infectious Disease Precautions

**Tuberculosis (TB):**
- Baseline TB test for all employees (two-step TST or IGRA)
- Annual TB testing for employees with patient contact
- Report known or suspected TB exposure immediately
- Refer patients with suspected TB for evaluation

**COVID-19 and Respiratory Illnesses:**
- Follow current CDC and local health department guidance
- Use appropriate PPE per current recommendations
- Stay home if symptomatic
- Report exposure to supervisor
- Follow testing and quarantine protocols per current guidance

**MRSA and Drug-Resistant Organisms:**
- Use standard precautions
- Hand hygiene critical
- Use gloves for contact with infected areas or contaminated items
- Dedicate equipment to patient when possible

**Influenza:**
- Annual flu vaccination strongly encouraged
- Stay home when experiencing flu symptoms
- Use respiratory hygiene/cough etiquette
- Mask if caring for patients while symptomatic (only if cannot stay home)

### Waste Disposal

**Regulated Medical Waste:**

**Definition:** Waste contaminated with blood or body fluids that could release when handled.

**Handling:**
- Use red bags or bags labeled with biohazard symbol
- Close securely when 3/4 full
- Do not compress or compact
- Keep bags upright to prevent leakage

**In Client Homes:**
- Small amounts of regulated waste (gloves, gauze): May dispose in client's trash in sealed plastic bag
- Sharps: Must be in approved sharps container
- Large amounts of waste: Bag separately and remove for proper disposal
- Follow facility/agency protocols

**Non-Regulated Waste:**
- Gloves and supplies not contaminated with free-flowing blood
- Can be disposed in regular trash
- Seal in plastic bag before disposal

### Infection Control in Client Home

**Cleaning and Disinfection:**
- Clean visibly soiled surfaces with detergent and water
- Disinfect surfaces contacted by blood or body fluids
- Use EPA-registered disinfectant or 1:10 bleach solution
- Allow proper contact time per product instructions
- Wear gloves during cleaning

**Equipment:**
- Dedicate equipment to individual patient when possible
- Clean and disinfect reusable equipment between patients
- Follow manufacturer instructions for cleaning
- Do not share blood glucose meters between patients

**Laundry:**
- Wear gloves when handling soiled linens
- Do not rinse soiled linens (increases exposure risk)
- Transport in leak-proof bag
- Client/family responsible for laundering in home
- If agency launders: Wash in hot water with detergent

### Training Requirements

**Initial Training (Before Patient Contact):**
- OSHA Bloodborne Pathogen Standard
- Infectious diseases and routes of transmission
- Use of personal protective equipment
- Hand hygiene
- Sharps safety
- Exposure incident procedure
- Hepatitis B vaccination information

**Annual Training (Every 12 Months):**
- Refresher on all initial topics
- Updates to standard or regulations
- New procedures or equipment
- Review of exposure incidents (de-identified)

**Documentation:**
- Training date and content
- Trainer name and qualifications
- Employee signature
- Maintain for duration of employment plus 30 years

## 6. VEHICLE AND DRIVING SAFETY

### Purpose

To prevent motor vehicle accidents and ensure safe driving practices for employees traveling to client homes.

### Scope

This policy applies to all employees who drive as part of their job duties, whether using company vehicles or personal vehicles.

### Driver Requirements

**Minimum Qualifications:**
- Valid driver's license for vehicle class operated
- Minimum age 18 (21 for some insurance policies)
- Clean driving record (no major violations in past 3 years)
- Current auto insurance (personal vehicles)

**Prohibited:**
- DUI/DWI conviction within past 5 years
- License suspension or revocation within past 3 years
- Multiple at-fault accidents (3+ in past 3 years)
- Reckless driving conviction within past 3 years

**Driver License Verification:**
- Employees must provide copy of valid driver license at hire
- License verification repeated annually
- Employees must report license suspension/revocation immediately
- Employees must report moving violations within 5 days

- Auto insurance meeting state minimum requirements
- Proof of insurance on file with employer
- Insurance verification updated every 6 months

**Company Vehicles (if applicable):**
- Regular maintenance schedule followed
- Daily pre-trip inspection required
- Defects reported immediately
- Vehicle use log maintained

### Safe Driving Practices

**Cell Phone Use - ZERO TOLERANCE:**

**PROHIBITED While Driving:**
- Texting or reading texts
- Emailing
- Social media use
- Internet browsing
- Handheld phone conversations
- Watching videos
- Taking photos

**ALLOWED:**
- Hands-free phone calls using Bluetooth or speaker
- Voice-activated GPS navigation
- Emergency 911 calls (pull over if possible)

**Policy:** If you need to use your phone for any prohibited purpose, you MUST safely pull over and park.

**Violation:** First violation may result in termination. This is a safety-critical rule.

**Distracted Driving:**

**Avoid:**
- Eating or drinking while driving
- Applying makeup or grooming
- Reading (maps, directions, documents)
- Reaching for objects
- Adjusting radio/controls while moving

**Best Practices:**
- Program GPS before driving
- Review route before departure
- Set climate controls before driving
- Secure loose items
- Avoid emotional or stressful phone conversations, even hands-free

**Defensive Driving:**

**Always:**
- Obey all traffic laws and posted speed limits
- Maintain safe following distance (3-4 seconds)
- Scan ahead and check mirrors frequently
- Signal all turns and lane changes
- Yield right-of-way when in doubt
- Adjust speed for weather and road conditions
- Stop completely at stop signs and red lights
- Come to complete stop before turning right on red

**Never:**
- Speed or drive aggressively
- Tailgate
- Weave through traffic
- Run yellow lights
- Roll through stop signs
- Drive while fatigued or drowsy
- Drive under influence of alcohol, drugs, or impairing medications

### Weather and Road Conditions

**Adverse Weather:**
- Reduce speed in rain, fog, ice, or snow
- Increase following distance
- Use headlights in rain, fog, or low visibility
- Do not use cruise control in wet or icy conditions
- If conditions are unsafe, delay travel or pull over safely

**Severe Weather Policy:**
- Employees not required to drive in unsafe conditions
- Notify supervisor if unable to reach client due to weather
- No discipline for weather-related delays
- Safety is priority over service delivery

**Flooding:**
- Never drive through flooded roadways
- Turn around, don't drown
- Find alternate route or delay visit

### Parking and Client Home Access

**Parking Safety:**
- Park in designated or safe areas only
- Avoid blocking driveways or mailboxes
- Park under streetlights when possible for evening visits
- Lock vehicle and take valuables with you
- Be aware of surroundings when exiting vehicle

**Arriving at Client Home:**
- Verify address before approaching
- Be aware of dogs or other hazards
- Walk carefully on sidewalks and steps
- Use handrails
- Knock or ring bell; do not enter unannounced

**Leaving Client Home:**
- Have keys ready before walking to vehicle
- Stay alert to surroundings
- Report any safety concerns about client location

### Seat Belt Policy

**Required:** Driver and all passengers must wear seat belts at all times when vehicle is in motion.

**No Exceptions:** Seat belt use is mandatory and non-negotiable.

### Accident Procedures

**If Involved in Motor Vehicle Accident:**

**Step 1: Safety First**
- Stop immediately (leaving scene is illegal)
- Turn on hazard lights
- Move vehicle to safe location if possible and if vehicle is drivable
- Check for injuries

**Step 2: Call Supervisor**
- Notify supervisor immediately (while at scene if possible)

  - Name, phone number, address

**Step 5: Complete Reports**

- Provide copies of:
  - Police report (when available)
  - Photos
  - Other driver information

## 7. VIOLENCE PREVENTION AND WORKPLACE SECURITY

### Purpose

To protect employees from violence, threatening behavior, and unsafe situations encountered in client homes and during work activities.

### Policy Statement

Leora Home Health is committed to providing a safe work environment free from violence, threats, and intimidation. We recognize that home health care workers may be vulnerable to violence from patients, family members, or others encountered during home visits.

Violence or threats of violence will not be tolerated and may result in immediate termination and criminal prosecution.

### Scope

This policy applies to violence or threats from:
- Patients/clients
- Family members or visitors in client homes
- Other household members
- Members of the public encountered during work

### Definitions

**Workplace Violence:** Any act or threat of physical violence, harassment, intimidation, or other threatening, disruptive behavior that occurs at the work site or during work activities.

**Types:**
- **Type I:** Criminal intent (no relationship to business or employees)
- **Type II:** Customer/client violence (patient or family member)
- **Type III:** Worker-on-worker (co-worker, supervisor, former employee)
- **Type IV:** Personal relationship (domestic violence spillover to workplace)

### Zero Tolerance Policy

**Prohibited Behaviors:**
- Physical assault or battery
- Threatening or intimidating behavior
- Verbal abuse or harassment
- Sexual harassment or assault
- Possession of weapons (unless legally authorized peace officer)
- Stalking
- Destruction of property
- Any behavior that creates fear or intimidation

**Applies To:**
- All employees
- All clients and family members
- Any person on company premises or client homes during work activities

### Pre-Service Violence Risk Assessment

**Initial Assessment:**

Before beginning services with new client, assess:

**Patient Factors:**
- [ ] History of violence or aggression
- [ ] Diagnosis of dementia or confusion
- [ ] Mental health conditions
- [ ] Substance abuse history or active use
- [ ] Poor impulse control
- [ ] Sexual aggression or inappropriate behavior
- [ ] Non-compliance or resistance to care

**Environmental Factors:**
- [ ] Weapons in home (firearms, knives, etc.)
- [ ] Weapons secured and stored safely
- [ ] Household members with concerning behaviors
- [ ] Substance abuse by household members
- [ ] Domestic violence history or indicators
- [ ] High-crime neighborhood
- [ ] Isolated location
- [ ] Poor lighting or unsafe access

**Risk Level Determination:**

**Low Risk:**
- No concerning factors identified
- Standard precautions adequate

**Moderate Risk:**
- Some concerning factors present
- Additional precautions required:
  - Detailed safety plan in care plan
  - Family/caregiver present during visits
  - Visit during daylight hours
  - Maintain clear path to exit
  - Check-in/check-out procedure with supervisor

**High Risk:**
- Multiple concerning factors
- History of violence
- Special precautions required:
  - Two-person visits required
  - Security escort if available
  - Limit visit duration
  - Maintain distance from patient
  - Consider termination of services if risks cannot be mitigated

**Documentation:**
All risk assessments and safety plans must be documented in patient record and communicated to all staff assigned to case.

### Employee Safety Procedures

**General Safety Practices:**

1. **Be Aware of Surroundings:**
   - Note exits before starting care
   - Position yourself with clear path to exit
   - Keep car keys and phone accessible
   - Be aware of people and activities in home

2. **Trust Your Instincts:**
   - If situation feels unsafe, leave
   - Do not ignore warning signs
   - It is always okay to leave if you feel threatened

3. **Professional Boundaries:**
   - Maintain professional relationship
   - Avoid personal topics that could escalate
   - Do not argue with patient or family
   - Stay calm and neutral

4. **Communication:**
   - Use calm, respectful tone
   - Give patient personal space
   - Explain what you're doing
   - Listen to concerns
   - Avoid confrontation

5. **Check-In Procedures:**
   - Contact office at start and end of each visit (for high-risk cases)
   - If required, establish "code word" for help needed
   - Keep phone charged and accessible

**Warning Signs of Escalating Behavior:**

Recognize early warning signs:
- Raised voice or yelling
- Angry or hostile tone
- Aggressive posture or gestures
- Pacing or restlessness
- Clenched fists
- Intense staring
- Verbal threats
- Throwing objects
- Invasion of personal space
- Blocking exit

**De-Escalation Techniques:**

If patient or family member appears agitated:

1. **Stay Calm:**
   - Control your voice tone and volume
   - Breathe slowly
   - Do not react emotionally

2. **Listen:**
   - Allow person to express concerns
   - Acknowledge their feelings
   - "I can see you're upset"

3. **Give Space:**
   - Maintain 6-8 foot distance
   - Do not touch agitated person
   - Respect personal space

4. **Be Respectful:**
   - Use calm, respectful language
   - Avoid arguing or contradicting
   - Do not criticize or judge

5. **Offer Options:**
   - Give person sense of control
   - "Would you like me to come back later?"
   - "Can we talk about what's bothering you?"

6. **Set Limits:**
   - Calmly state acceptable behavior
   - "I want to help you, but I cannot stay if you continue yelling"
   - "I need you to step back"

7. **Seek Help:**
   - Call supervisor if situation not improving
   - Call 911 if threatened or in danger
   - Leave if you feel unsafe

**When to Leave Immediately:**

Leave the premises immediately if:
- Person makes threats of violence
- Person displays weapon
- Person physically assaults you or attempts to
- Person blocks your exit
- Situation is escalating despite de-escalation attempts
- You feel you are in immediate danger
- Your instinct tells you to leave

**Procedure:**
1. Leave premises calmly but quickly
2. Go to safe location (car, neighbor's home, public place)
3. Call 911 if needed
4. Call supervisor immediately
5. Do not return to location

### Weapons in Client Homes

**Firearms and Weapons:**
- Patients have right to own firearms in their homes
- Firearms must be stored securely (locked, unloaded, separate from ammunition)
- Firearms should not be visible or accessible during care visit
- Employee has right to refuse service if weapons are unsecured or displayed in threatening manner

1. Stay calm
2. Do not argue
3. Leave immediately if possible

5. Notify supervisor
6. Services will be terminated

### Sexual Harassment and Assault

**Definition:** Unwelcome sexual advances, requests for sexual favors, or other verbal/physical conduct of sexual nature.

- Sexual comments or jokes
- Questions about sexual activities
- Inappropriate touching
- Exposure of private body parts

### Domestic Violence Situations

## 8. PERSONAL PROTECTIVE EQUIPMENT (PPE)

### Purpose

To ensure employees have access to and properly use personal protective equipment to prevent workplace injuries and exposures.

### Policy Statement

Leora Home Health will provide appropriate personal protective equipment to all employees at no cost. Employees are required to use PPE as directed for their protection.

### Employer Responsibilities

**Provide:**
- Appropriate PPE for tasks performed
- PPE in appropriate sizes
- Training on proper use
- Replacement of worn or damaged PPE
- Cleaning and maintenance of reusable PPE

**At No Cost to Employee:**
- All PPE provided free of charge
- Replacements provided as needed
- No deduction from pay for PPE

### Employee Responsibilities

**Required:**
- Use PPE as instructed
- Wear PPE for entire duration of exposure
- Inspect PPE before use
- Report damaged or inadequate PPE
- Clean and store reusable PPE properly
- Replace disposable PPE after each use
- Complete training on PPE use

### Types of PPE and When to Use

#### 1. Gloves

**Types Available:**
- Disposable nitrile gloves (latex-free)
- Vinyl gloves (for non-contact tasks)
- Utility gloves (for cleaning)
- Cold weather gloves (for winter outdoor work)

**When Required:**
- Contact with blood or body fluids
- Contact with mucous membranes or non-intact skin
- Handling contaminated items
- Cleaning contaminated surfaces
- Wound care
- Handling sharps
- Potential exposure to infectious materials

**Proper Use:**
- Select appropriate size for good fit
- Inspect for tears or holes before use
- Pull snugly over wrists
- Remove gloves if torn during use and replace
- Never wash or reuse disposable gloves
- Remove properly to avoid self-contamination
- Dispose immediately after use
- Wash hands immediately after removing gloves

**When NOT to Wear:**
- When not necessary (over-gloving reduces hand hygiene)
- Do not wear gloves in hallways or between patients
- Remove gloves before touching phone, computer, doorknobs outside patient area

#### 2. Masks and Respirators

**Types Available:**
- Surgical/procedure masks
- N95 respirators (fit-tested)
- KN95 masks
- Face shields

**When Required:**
- Potential for splashes/sprays of blood or body fluids
- Care of patients with respiratory infections
- Aerosol-generating procedures
- As required during disease outbreaks (COVID-19, etc.)
- Per current health department guidance

**Surgical Mask Use:**
- Covers nose and mouth completely
- Secure ties or ear loops
- Pinch metal nosepiece for good fit
- Do not touch mask while wearing
- Change if wet or soiled
- Dispose after use (do not reuse)
- Wash hands after removing

**N95 Respirator Use:**
- Must be fit-tested before first use
- Only use respirator that has been fit-tested for you
- Check seal before each use:
  - Place hands over respirator
  - Inhale sharply - should feel suction
  - Exhale - should feel resistance
  - If air leaks around edges, readjust
- Follow same guidelines as surgical mask
- May be reused per manufacturer and health department guidelines during shortages
- Store in breathable paper bag between uses if reusing

**Face Shield:**
- Used in addition to mask (not instead of)
- Covers face from forehead to below chin
- Extends to ears on sides
- Clean and disinfect after each use
- Inspect for cracks or damage

#### 3. Eye Protection

**Types Available:**
- Safety goggles
- Safety glasses with side shields
- Face shields

**When Required:**
- Potential for splashes/sprays to eyes
- During procedures that may generate droplets
- Wound irrigation
- Suctioning
- When caring for patients with respiratory infections

**Proper Use:**
- Must fit snugly against face or forehead
- Regular eyeglasses are NOT adequate (no side protection)
- Prescription glasses may be worn under goggles
- Clean and disinfect after each use
- Replace if scratched or damaged (impairs vision)

#### 4. Gowns and Aprons

**Types Available:**
- Disposable fluid-resistant gowns
- Disposable plastic aprons
- Reusable cloth gowns (limited situations)

**When Required:**
- Potential for large-scale contamination of clothing
- Procedures likely to generate splashes
- Large amounts of blood or body fluids expected
- Care of patients with certain infectious diseases

**Proper Use:**
- Put on gown before other PPE
- Secure at neck and waist
- Covers torso from neck to knees
- Sleeves to end of wrists
- Remove carefully to avoid self-contamination:
  1. Unfasten ties
  2. Peel away from body, touching only inside
  3. Roll into bundle
  4. Dispose in appropriate container
- Do not wear outside of patient care area

#### 5. Shoe Covers

**Types Available:**
- Disposable shoe covers

**When Required:**
- Entry into homes with specific contamination concerns
- As directed by supervisor for specific situations

**Proper Use:**
- Pull over shoes, covering completely
- Secure at top
- Remove before exiting contaminated area
- Dispose properly

#### 6. Safety Shoes

**When Recommended:**
- For staff who may be exposed to:
  - Sharp objects on floors
  - Heavy equipment
  - Wet floors

**Requirements:**
- Closed-toe shoes required for all patient care staff
- Non-slip soles recommended
- Comfortable for walking and standing

### PPE for Specific Tasks

| Task | Required PPE |
|------|--------------|
| Basic patient care (no exposure expected) | None required (use good judgment) |
| Bathing/hygiene assistance | Gloves |
| Incontinence care | Gloves, gown/apron if large amount of fluid |
| Wound care | Gloves; mask and eye protection if spraying/splashing possible |
| Injections/fingersticks | Gloves |
| IV therapy | Gloves |
| Oral care | Gloves |
| Emptying drainage bags | Gloves |
| Cleaning contaminated surfaces | Gloves, other PPE as appropriate |
| Handling soiled linens | Gloves |
| Respiratory care (suctioning, etc.) | Gloves, mask, eye protection, gown |

### PPE Donning (Putting On) Sequence

**Proper Order:**
1. **Gown** (if required)
   - Fully cover torso
   - Secure at neck and waist
2. **Mask or Respirator**
   - Secure ties or elastic
   - Fit to nose bridge
   - Fit-check respirator
3. **Eye Protection**
   - Position over eyes and adjust to fit
4. **Gloves**
   - Select correct size
   - Pull over gown cuffs if wearing gown

### PPE Doffing (Removing) Sequence

**Proper Order (to avoid self-contamination):**
1. **Gloves**
   - Most contaminated item
   - Remove first
   - Peel off carefully without touching skin
   - Dispose immediately
2. **Gown** (if worn)
   - Unfasten ties
   - Pull away from neck and shoulders
   - Turn inside out as removing
   - Roll into bundle
   - Dispose
3. **Wash Hands or Use Hand Sanitizer**
4. **Eye Protection**
   - Handle by headband or ear pieces only
   - Do not touch front
   - Place in designated receptacle for cleaning
5. **Mask or Respirator**
   - Handle by ties or elastic only
   - Do not touch front
   - Dispose
6. **Wash Hands**
   - Final hand washing required

**IMPORTANT:** Wash or sanitize hands after removing each piece of PPE if hands become contaminated, and always after removing all PPE.

### Fit Testing (N95 Respirators)

**Who Needs Fit Testing:**
- Employees who may be required to wear N95 respirators

**Frequency:**
- Initial fit test before first use
- Annual fit test
- New fit test if:
  - Different model or size respirator
  - Significant weight change (+ or - 20 lbs)
  - Facial changes (dental work, scarring)
  - Fit test failure

**Procedure:**
- Performed by qualified person
- Quantitative or qualitative fit test
- Test multiple respirator models/sizes to find best fit
- Employee may only use respirator models/sizes they have been fit-tested for

**Facial Hair:**
- Facial hair interferes with respirator seal
- Employees required to wear N95 must be clean-shaven in seal area
- Beard, mustache, stubble, or sideburns that cross seal area are prohibited

**Conditions That May Affect Respirator Use:**

- Claustrophobia
- Anxiety
